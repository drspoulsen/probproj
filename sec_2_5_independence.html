Section 2.5 Independence

Look at the formal definition of the word independence. Think about this definition and its meaning in mathematics.

Definition 2.5.1.

Not depending on something else for strength or effectiveness; freestanding

How do you think this relates to probability?

Example 2.5.2.

Suppose you roll a fair six-sided dice and it’s a four. Will this affect your next roll? Are you more likely to get a four? Less likely?

Solution.

The first roll should not affect the second roll. We should be equally like to get a four on the second roll as we did the first. This is because these are two independent events.

Definition 2.5.3.

Two events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent if

\begin{equation*} P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B) \end{equation*}

To relate back the example we had earlier, think of the first roll as ‘Event A’ and the second as ‘Event B’.

In the previous section we talked about conditional probability. The probability of some event \(A\) given some event \(B\text{.}\) If events are independent, since one does not affect the other, we can define the conditional probability

\begin{equation*} P(A|B)=P(A) \end{equation*}

Furthermore,

\begin{equation*} P(B|A)=P(B) \end{equation*}

Note that this is not a definition for independence. The probability of “\(A\) given \(B\)” is not the converse of “\(B\) given \(A\text{.}\)”

Refer back to the previous example on Section 2.4. The probability of getting snake eyes, given we already rolled a one, is equal to the probability of just rolling a one.

Example 2.5.4.

Suppose \(A\) is the event you roll an even number and \(B\) is the event you roll a four. Are these events independent?

Solution.

\(P(A \cap B) \neq P(A)P(B)\text{.}\) Not independent!

How do you think the formal definition of independence would extend to three independent events A, B, and C?

Solution.
\begin{equation*} P(A\cap B\cap C)=P(A)P(B)P(C) \end{equation*}

It is also true that if three events are independent, for any combination of two events, independence still holds such that

\begin{equation*} P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} P(B\cap C)=P(B)P(C) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} P(A\cap C)=P(A)P(C) \end{equation*}
